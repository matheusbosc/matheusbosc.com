About Me

Matheus is a high school student who splits his time between competitive rowing, volleyball, saxophone, and building things — games, software, and a homelab that doubles as his personal testing ground. After a $5k game jam, he co-founded his own studio to bring their game to life, on track for a 2027 release.

My Story

My interest in game development started in elementary school, inspired largely by Minecraft. I chased that spark through tutorial after tutorial, quitting more times than I can count — until eventually, I stopped quitting for good.

For a while, games were the only software I made. But the routine eventually wore on me, and I branched out — starting with a TODO app for CS50, my first taste of writing straight code instead of working through a game engine's UI. I kept alternating between the two, eventually picking up Chat++ to learn C++ and networking from scratch. Now I move between games and general software depending on what I want to learn next.

My homelab started almost by accident — I got tired of doing something manually, so I set up Jellyfin to automate it. That one server turned into several: Vaultwarden, Portainer, Calibre-web, and more, each one solving a small problem or scratching a curiosity.

What I Do Now

These days, my work spans a few different areas of software. I spend most of my time in game development with Unity and C#, but I also build general software in Python, and tinker with cloud and DevOps tools like Docker, Cloudflare, GitHub Actions, and Jenkins. Video editing rounds out the mix, mostly for devlogs and studio content.

Why I Do It


Why do I make games & software?

I love creating — building something out of nothing has always appealed to me. Combining that with computers, one of my other big passions, felt natural. Beyond the act of creating itself, I'm drawn to the problem-solving side of it: every bug, every design decision, every "how do I make this work" moment is its own small puzzle.


Why do I explore cloud and DevOps?

Mostly independence. I like being able to host and run my own services — whether that's a tool I use daily or infrastructure for a project — without depending on someone else's pricing, privacy policy, or uptime. If I can build it myself and control it, I usually will.


How do I approach learning & problem-solving?

I tend to start from the end. I picture the finished result — what it should look like, how it should behave — and then work backwards, reconstructing each piece needed to get there. It forces me to understand why each part exists, not just how to copy-paste it into place.
