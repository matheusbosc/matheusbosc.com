## The Constraint

Last Ember came out of a game jam, built with a small team. My role covered both development and art — and it was the art that ended up eating most of the clock.

Everything was hand-drawn, digitally, and stylized to look like paper. It came out looking exactly the way I wanted — genuinely one of the best-looking things I've made — but that quality had a cost. Every asset took real time to get right, which meant the programming side got squeezed into whatever time was left.

## Designing Around Less Time

With the clock working against us, the approach was simple: break the game down to its core building blocks, and build *those* well instead of spreading thin across a longer feature list.

- Not every planned feature made it in
- The features that *did* make it in got the attention they needed to actually work well

## The Feature I'm Proudest Of

The standout system, and the one I put the most care into, was the **event system**. Random events would trigger during a run and cause your fire to burn out faster — pushing you closer to losing:

- Rain
- Storms
- Wind

Each one added pressure in a different way, and together they made every run feel a little different depending on what the game threw at you. It was the piece that made the "survive as long as you can" loop actually feel alive instead of static.

## What I Learned

Working under a hard deadline, split between two very different disciplines (art and code), taught me a lot about prioritization — figuring out what actually needed to be perfect versus what just needed to work. It also meant working closely with a team under pressure, which was its own kind of practice: going from a rough prototype to something that felt like a finished game, together, in a short window of time.
