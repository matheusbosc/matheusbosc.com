## The Course Behind It

This one didn't come from a jam or a class — it came from a course I asked Claude to build for me. I wanted to actually learn assembly and get a real understanding of systems design, so I asked for something structured like a college course: lectures, problem sets, assignments, tests, exams, the whole format. Mini-WC was the final project for that course.

## What It Does

Mini-WC is exactly what it sounds like — a stripped-down version of the Linux `wc` utility, written in x86 assembly using FASM. Real `wc` counts lines, words, characters, and bytes. Mine only counts bytes — hence *mini*.

Small scope, on purpose. At this level, even "just count bytes" is a real exercise in low-level programming, with none of the abstractions a higher-level language would normally hide from you.

## The Hard Part

Assembly doesn't forgive vague debugging. There's no stepping through readable variable names or clean error messages — just registers, memory addresses, and instructions.

- **Debugging** meant going line by line, watching what was actually sitting in each register at every step, and piecing together what the code was doing versus what I intended it to do. Hours of that.
- **Efficiency** was its own battle — early versions had plenty of redundant lines doing work that didn't need to happen at all.
- **Memory leaks** showed up more than once, and each one had to be tracked down and fixed by hand, since nothing here cleans up after you automatically.

## Why It Mattered

Mini-WC is small in scope, but it's the project that actually forced me to understand what's happening underneath the languages I normally write in — registers, memory, and the raw mechanics of a program instead of the abstractions built on top of them.
